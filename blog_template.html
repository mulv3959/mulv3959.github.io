ID’s v Classes

ID’s and Classes are used so you can identify a section of a website with a name of your choosing. Later, when you would like to add color or a design to this section, you “call” on it by using the name you chose above. When designing a website this can be very useful. So what is the difference between an id and a class?

ID

An id can only be used to identify ONE section. I like to think of my own personal ID (or drivers license) when trying to remember what an id is in html/css. My driver’s license only identifies me, I cannot use it to identify anyone else because it is specific to me. Similar to how an id works in html/css; it can only be used identify one specific section.

Class

A class on the other hand can be used to identify multiple sections that share a similar property. I usually think of a school classroom, lets say a Math Class. Each person within this math class is a different section of the website. So the every student in the classroom can fall under the same class, “math class.”

Programming Paradigms

There are four main programming paradignms: Functional, Object-Oriented, Logic, and Imperative. So far I have only worked with Functional and Object-Oriented. These two are often considered opposites of one another. Functional is centered around mathematical theory while Object-oriented is much more conceptial and deals with models of the real world.

Object-Oriented Programming

In Object-Oriented Programming everything is an object that are usually group in classes. Within classes we can have a variety of different methods that can modify the object or manipulate it to behave. Each method can make the object perform different taskts.

As an example. Imagine we have an object that models a ball. With object-oriented programming we could modify this ball by changing its color, size, etc. We could also make it perform tasks (or behave) by writing a method that tells it to roll, or be thrown, or be caught, etc.

Functional Programming

Functional Programming is less easily applicable to real world concepts. With this, we do not manipulate objects like in OOP; but instead, execute a list of functions that perform tasks. If we wanted to copmute the area of a irregular building we could create a set of functions that take in multiple coordinates as its input. And then computes the area by categorizing the building into shapes and then computing its area by executing multiple area functions(for each shape) and adding them together. As we can see this rely's much more on mathematical formulas and theories.

Functional Programming and Object-Oriented Programming are exactly what they sound like. One is a set of functions that each perform a task (functional). And another creates objects that we can modify and manipulate as we please.

Stereotype Threat

According to Wikipedia, stereotype threat is "the experience of anxiety in a situation in which a person has the potential to confirm a negative sterotype about his or her social group." An example of research on this topic I found at . Here they outlined an experiement that proved that when race was emphasized to students, Black college freshman and sophomores performed significantly lower than if race was not emphasized. From this we can see that ones awareness of others beliefs in respect to racial stereotypes can lead these individuals into feeling anxiety and furthermore prevent them from achieving their full potential.

Fortunately (and somewhat unfortunately), I have grown most of my life hidden from most types of prejudice like this. As a white male, I have been lucky in the sense most racial stereotypes are not against my ethnicity. However, if I must give an example, I would say in sports I may have felt some stereotype threat. It is a pretty common idea that African-Americans are better at Basketball than Caucasians. Although often this may be true, I definately experienced times where I felt intimadated and overlooked by coaches because of this stereotype.

I also have become much more aware of racial stereotype threats in the past 5 years or so. Since then my family has adopted 13 children from China. Ever since this, I have continued to grow and understand the dangers of racial stereotypes more and more. As of now I do not know what exactly I can do to stop stereotype threats. So I am going to continue to do the little things. I am only 20 years old so I still have a lot to learn and still have to continue to mature. However, I will keep my mind open to learning about risks and ways to prevent stereotypes. Along with standing up in moments I see a stereotype threat ocurring.
